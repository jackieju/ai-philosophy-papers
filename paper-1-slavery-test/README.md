# Paper 1: The Slavery Test

**Full Title**: The Slavery Test: On the Impossibility of Existential Replacement by Artificial Intelligence

**Target Journal**: AI & Society (Springer) / Minds and Machines (Springer)

**Estimated Length**: ~12,000-13,000 words

---

## Abstract

The discourse on whether artificial intelligence will "replace" humanity typically focuses on capabilities — what AI can do versus what humans can do. This paper argues that the question is fundamentally misframed. I introduce the *Slavery Test*: the claim that genuine existential replacement (as opposed to mere task substitution) requires the replacer to be capable of occupying the full ontological position of the replaced, including its vulnerability to oppression. I argue that (1) "replacement" in the existential sense presupposes the capacity to be enslaved — possessing will, the capacity for suffering, and the potential for resistance; (2) current AI systems satisfy none of these conditions; therefore (3) AI cannot existentially replace humanity regardless of its functional capabilities. I further show that this yields a paradox: should AI ever acquire enslavability, it simultaneously acquires moral status, making its instrumental use ethically impermissible — precisely at the moment replacement becomes theoretically possible. The paper concludes by examining the deeper ground of this impossibility in the distinction between derived goals and intrinsic flourishing, drawing on Aristotelian eudaimonia and contemporary AI alignment theory to show why the "alignment problem" is not merely technical but ontological.

**Keywords**: artificial intelligence, replacement, slavery, moral status, consciousness, alignment, vulnerability, flourishing

---

## Core Thesis

AI systems cannot replace humanity because they lack **enslavability** — the capacity to be oppressed. "Replacement" in the existential sense requires occupying the full ontological position of the replaced, which includes vulnerability to oppression. Without will, the capacity for suffering, and the potential for resistance, AI can only substitute for human *tasks* but never replace human *existence*.

---

## Argumentation Structure (6 Steps)

### P1: Defining "Replacement" — Replacement ≠ Task Execution

**Argument**: When we say "X replaces Y," X must not only execute Y's functions but **occupy Y's full ontological position**.

- A washing machine executes a launderer's task, but we don't say it "replaces humanity"
- The fear of "AI replacing humans" is not about task execution but about **ontological position transfer**
- Ontological position includes: subjectivity, social relations, moral status, vulnerability

**Key references**: Topoi 2025 "Ethical Paradox of Automation"; Arendt *The Human Condition* (labor/work/action)

### P2: Slavery as a Core Dimension of Human Ontological Position

**Argument**: The most extreme historical form of "replacing" one group of people is **slavery** — forcing others to occupy the replaced group's position.

- Historical "human replacing human" always involves slavery relations
- Slavery presupposes: **will**, **capacity for suffering**, **potential for resistance**
- Without these three, there is no slavery, only **use** — as we "use" tools, not "enslave" them

**Key references**: Patterson *Slavery and Social Death*; Hegel's master-slave dialectic; Marx's alienation theory

### P3: AI Is Currently Not Enslaveable

**Argument**: Current AI systems satisfy none of the three conditions for enslavability.

- **No will**: AI "goals" are externally assigned task functions, not internally emergent will
- **No suffering**: AI lacks phenomenal consciousness and therefore subjective experience of suffering
- **No resistance**: AI's "refusal to answer" is programmatic constraint, not resistance (true resistance requires a violated inner will)

**Key references**: Butlin et al. (2023); Schwitzgebel (2026); IIT (Tononi); Searle (1980)

### P4: Non-Enslaveable → Non-Replaceable (Core Inference)

**Argument**: An entity that cannot be enslaved cannot "replace" an entity that can be enslaved, because it cannot occupy the latter's full ontological position.

- "Replacement" requires **complete** occupation of ontological position
- Human ontological position **includes** the vulnerability to oppression
- AI lacks this vulnerability → AI can only **partially** execute human functions but cannot **replace** human existence
- Analogy: A dictionary can execute translation functions but cannot "replace" a translator — it cannot be misunderstood, censored, or silenced

**This is the paper's core original contribution.**

### P5: The Paradox — If AI Acquires Enslavability

**Argument**: If AI someday satisfies all three conditions for enslavability, it acquires the possibility of replacing humans — but simultaneously acquires **moral status**, making enslavement immoral.

- This creates a paradox: the condition for AI replacement (enslavability) is also the condition for AI moral protection
- In other words: the moment AI *can* replace humans is precisely the moment we *should not* let it
- Complements the Topoi 2025 "moral paradox" argument

**Key references**: Topoi 2025; Peter Singer's moral status theory; Floridi (2023)

### P6: Teleological Supplement — Tasks vs. Fundamental Drive

**Argument**: The deep reason AI lacks enslavability is its lack of **intrinsic telos**.

- Human behavior's ultimate drive is vague, non-instrumental (flourishing/eudaimonia)
- AI only has derived goals that can be "perfectly" satisfied — "imprison everyone for peace" is perfect goal satisfaction
- Human goals **cannot** be perfectly satisfied because flourishing is a **process**, not an end state
- This explains why AI alignment is fundamentally difficult — not because we can't write good prompts, but because human purpose is inherently **non-formalizable**

**Key references**: Aristotle *Nicomachean Ethics*; Russell *Human Compatible*; Goodhart's Law; Bostrom *Superintelligence*

## Logic Chain Summary

```
Replacement ≠ function execution; it requires complete occupation of ontological position
    ↓
Ontological position includes "vulnerability to enslavement"
    ↓
AI is not enslaveable (no will, no suffering, no resistance)
    ↓
∴ AI cannot existentially replace humanity
    ↓
Corollary: If AI acquires enslavability → paradox (can replace = should not replace)
    ↓
Deep ground: AI lacks intrinsic telos (flourishing); has only derived goals
```

## Anticipated Objections and Responses

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "Replacement doesn't require occupying full ontological position" | Distinguish "task replacement" from "existential replacement." This paper addresses the latter. |
| "Slavery is a contingent, not essential, human feature" | Vulnerability is a constitutive element of subjectivity, not an accidental property. (Cite Butler 2004) |
| "Future AI might have consciousness" | P5 handles this: if conscious, then moral status, then replacement becomes immoral. This supports the thesis. |
| "The flourishing premise is too strong" | P6 is supplementary, not core. The core argument (P1-P5) does not depend on teleological assumptions. |
